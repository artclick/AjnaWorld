What your toddler needs
to learn

🖐️

Hands First

They learn through touch before anything else. Let them feel, squeeze, dig and explore before naming anything.

🔁

Repetition

Doing the same thing 20 times isn't boring — it's how their brain builds the connection. Follow their lead.

💬

Your Words

Name what they touch. "Cow. Big cow. The cow says moo." Simple, specific, attached to what they're doing right now.

⏱️

Short Sessions

5–10 minutes of full engagement is perfect. Don't extend past their interest. End while they still want more.

🌟

The Golden Rule

They don't need lessons. They need you beside them, present and curious, while they explore. Your calm attention is the most developmental thing in the room.

Montessori ideas

Simple activities
with big impact

🔓

Open & Close Lids

Tupperware containers, spice jars, small boxes. At this age, mechanisms are fascinating. Let them open, close, open again. Don't rush. Every open-close is a fine motor win.

🪣

Fill & Empty

Two bowls, any small objects — pasta, stones, balls. Transfer from one to the other with hands. This is early maths: full, empty, more, less. Narrate as he goes.

🪆

Nest & Stack

Stacking cups in both directions — up and inside each other. The nesting direction is harder and newer. Model it slowly, let them try.

📮

Object Permanence

The DoubleYX ball drop — they drop the ball through the hole, it disappears, reappears below. The disappearing object is deeply satisfying. They'll run this experiment over and over. That repetition is the whole lesson.

🔩

Nuts & Bolts

From the tool set — large wooden nuts on bolts. You start the thread, they turn to tighten. A completely new wrist rotation movement they haven't encountered yet.

📚

Language Basket

3 farm animal figurines on a small tray. Pick up the cow, say "cow," make the sound. Let them hold it. Put it down. That's the whole activity. Do it 3x a week, 3 different animals at a time.

Sensory Play Ideas

01

Chocolate Mud Farm

Cocoa powder + cornstarch + water = mud. Hide sheep and pig figurines inside. They dig them out with their hands. Name each animal as it appears. Add natural stones for texture contrast — smooth stones against the mud is a rich sensory pairing.

02

Milk Sensory Tray

Full-fat cow's milk in the white salad bowl. Froth with a handheld frother — completely mouth safe. Add cow figurines. Small cups for pouring. "The cow makes milk. White milk. Pour the milk." Connects animal to food.

03

Oobleck Dig

Cornstarch + water + green food colouring = farm field. Animals half-buried inside. They excavate. The oobleck behaves like both solid and liquid — genuinely surprising every time they squeeze it.

04

Dry Rice Discovery

A bowl of dry rice or oats, animals buried inside. Different texture from oobleck — dry, light, rustling sound. He scoops, pours, finds animals. Simpler setup, great for a quick weekday activity.

🎨 Magnetic Wall Ideas

  • Tape a large A3 green paper sheet to the wall at their height — instant farm field for the figurines to stand against
  • Finger paint directly on A3 paper taped to the wall — vertical surface painting uses different muscles and feels different from tray painting
  • Place 2 animal magnets on the wall, name them, let them move them around — the magnetic click is cause and effect they love
  • Draw a simple barn outline on paper, tape to wall — put the animals "inside" and "outside" the barn. In, out, more, less.
Art

First mark-making

✋

Finger Painting

One colour only. Tape an A3 sheet to the wall or high chair tray. No brush — just hands. They move their hand, colour appears. That cause and effect is the whole lesson. Don't direct, don't demonstrate too much. Just be beside them.

Try: dark paper + light paint. The contrast makes their marks more dramatic and satisfying.
🖍️

Honeysticks Crayons

One crayon at a time. Fist grip is correct at this age. Tape paper to the magnetic wall at standing height. Make marks alongside them on your own paper.

🎨

Coloured Paper

The IKEA coloured paper — try dark green or brown paper for the farm theme. Even before he marks it, naming the colour is language basket work.

Play

Blocks &
construction

🦊

Woodland Blockables

Fox, beaver, grey base. They can remove the head but not insert it yet — that's the challenge to work on this month. You align the peg above the hole, they push down. Celebrate every success. Fade your help gradually week by week.

Fine motor · ZPD
🧩

Farm Hide & Seek Puzzle

Lift the piece, find the hidden animal underneath, name it. They'll lift pieces but won't replace them correctly yet — that's fine. Lifting and naming is the whole activity for June. Replacing comes later. The reveal moment is the reward.

Object permanence · Farm vocabulary
🎵

Xylophone & Musical Instruments

Follow their lead with the xylophone — strike different bars, hear different sounds. Tap a rhythm and wait for them to copy. The rattles and shakers are cause and effect — shake, hear the sound, shake again. Music is early maths: pattern, rhythm, loud and quiet.

Music · Pattern · Cause & effect
🌈

Coloured Cloth Play

The sheer translucent coloured cloths are surprisingly rich for this age. Float one over their head — peekaboo with colour. Lay them flat and place farm animals on top — the colour shows through underneath. Hold one up to the light and watch their face as the colour changes the room. Scrunch, wave, drag — each action produces a different visual and tactile result. Name the colours as you go: red cloth, blue cloth. Simple, free, genuinely engaging.

Sensory · Colour vocabulary · Peekaboo
🔷

4-in-1 Sorting Box

Four activities in one: shape sorting, ball drop, peg insertion, ring ramp. Start with ball drop — immediately accessible and deeply satisfying. Shape sorting next. Don't introduce all four at once.

Shape sorting · Fine motor
🥚

Egg Toys

They're still working on fitting the two halves together. The trial and error persistence is the developmental work — not the result. Let them struggle productively. Only help if he's genuinely frustrated, not just working hard.

Spatial reasoning · Persistence
Gross motor

Moving his
whole body

🪨

Stapelstein Balance Stones

Brand new this month — a rainbow set with a balance board. At 13 months: place one stone flat on the floor, hold their hands, let him step on and off. The curved surface is wobbly and completely new for their feet and balance system. Don't stack them yet. One stone, stepping on and off, is the whole activity. Gradually reduce how much you hold their hands over the coming weeks. The balance board comes later — once they're confident on a single stone.

Balance · Proprioception · Body confidence
🪜

Stairs — Daily

They'll love climbing stairs every day and loves it. Keep this going — it's one of the best gross motor activities available at this age. Going up comes first. Work on coming down too: facing the stairs, one knee at a time. Stay close, let them do the work. Vary the stairs where possible — building stairs, park stairs, outdoor steps all feel different underfoot.

Leg strength · Bilateral coordination · Confidence
🔺

Pikler Triangle

The Pikler is one of the most underrated gross motor tools at this age — it builds upper body strength, risk assessment, and the specific confidence that comes from navigating height independently. To make it irresistible: drape a coloured cloth over one side to create a den underneath (they'll want to crawl in). Place a favourite farm animal at the top rung as a target to reach. Sit on the other side and play peekaboo through the rungs. The climbing is the activity — the play around it is the invitation.

Climbing · Risk assessment · Upper body strength
🚲

Bike Rides

This is one of the richest developmental activities available — and it looks like just a ride. Research on vestibular development shows that passive movement through varied environments activates the balance system, spatial reasoning, and depth perception simultaneously. Their brain is processing the moving world, the wind, the sounds, the changing terrain — all at once. Aim for 20–30 minutes with a destination that has a sensory reward: sandpit, water feature, open grass to crawl on. Narrate what you pass: "big tree, red bus, hear the birds." The destination matters as much as the journey.

Vestibular · Visual-spatial · Outdoor · Language
🏊

Weekly Swimming

The most time-sensitive physical activity of this entire year. Research shows that children have a natural openness to water confidence between 12–18 months — before fear of water is learned and before the dive reflex diminishes. Every week in the pool during this window is building something that becomes significantly harder to develop after 18 months. In lessons this month: back float practice, intentional submersion with a cue word before each dip, and wall grab. No float jackets — these teach the wrong body position and build false confidence without real skill. Parent in the water, hands supporting horizontally, fading support gradually session by session. Goal by end of June: comfortable face in water and a back float with light support.

Water confidence · Bilateral coordination · Breath control · Time-sensitive

The commentator rule

Don't create special maths time. Just narrate what's already happening with precise words. When they fill the bowl with rice — "full bowl." When they dump it — "all gone, empty." When they pick up the big cow — "big cow." The maths is already everywhere. Your words are the only thing to add.
